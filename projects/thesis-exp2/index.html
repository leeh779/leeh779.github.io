You are now exploring web badges in 90s. Back in the days, online users displayed web badges on the bottom of their personal websites. Different communities and platforms gave those badges to reward members of the community. To reward supporters, they granted a little badge for them to express values that they stand for.



Squash Email Spiders is a community to combat Email Spider Programs. Email Spider Programs go from site to site, zoom through newsgroups, and gobble up message boards with fervor. These are programs that collect thousands of addys for their user, giving him a mailing list that he can send his spam junk mail to. Supporters of this community displayed the badge to let people know about the method to stop spammers.

The HTML Writers Guild is the world's largest international organization of Web authors with over 147,000 members in more than 150 nations worldwide. It's a unique community that provides resources, support, representation, and education for web authors at all skill levels. The badge is given for members of the guild, who are interested in web design and development.

Members of the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) blue ribbon campaign used this badge to support the essential human right of free speech, a fundamental building block of free society, affirmed by the U.S. Bill of Rights in 1791 and by the U.N. Declaration of Human Rights in 1948. EFF is a donor-funded nonprofit group of passionate people who depend on your support to continue successfully defending your digital rights.

Badge for members of I Hate Frames Club. This community shares idea that frames are simply a bad way to design a webpage. Frame is one way to design a web page. It takes longer to download, it almost always makes a site more confusing, it is especially annoying on small monitors because they waste valuable browser space, printing a webpage is difficult, and it is prone to crash browsers.

Badge for Golden Key Campaign. Concerned with the security of electronic communication, the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) promoted a campaign for online privacy.
